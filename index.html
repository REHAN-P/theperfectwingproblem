Introduction

For my MCV4U culminating assessment, I was tasked to research and create my own optimization problem using the ideas and techniques we learned in this course. Since I have an interest in aerospace, aviation, and engineering, I wanted to choose a topic that connects real world aircraft designs with the type of calculus we study in Grade 12. After looking into different possibilities, I decided to focus on something that is important in aviation but can also relate to grade 12 calculus optimization problems, which is finding the most efficient angle of attack for an airplane wing. It's more commonly called the "Perfect Wing" problem and this idea comes from real aerodynamics research, where engineers study how a wing's angle of attack affects the lift it produces and the drag it goes through.

Through my research, I learned that the lift to drag ratio is one of the most important measurements of how well a wing performs, especially during cruising flight. A higher lift to drag ratio means the aircraft can fly farther while using less fuel, which is a major goal for aerospace engineers to achieve when designing aircrafts. I also learned that lift and drag do not increase at the same rate as the angle of attack changes. Lift increases in a mostly linear way, while drag increases way faster. This creates a sweet spot where the wing is most efficient. Engineers typically use advanced physics and calculus to find this point, but I will simplify the relationships so they fit within Grade 12 calculus while still staying pretty realistic.

To turn this into my own high-school-level optimization problem, I will use simplified equations for lift and drag that still show the same behaviour as real wings. This lets me use the derivative rules we learned in class to find the angle that gives the best lift to drag ratio. My goal is to show how calculus can be used to solve a real engineering problem in a way that is understandable to grade 12 students.

The Problem

A small propeller plane is cruising at constant speed. The pilot can adjust the angle of attack $\alpha$ of the wing, affecting the lift and drag forces. Engineers measure wing efficiency using the lift-to-drag ratio.

The lift and drag forces (in N) are modeled by:
$L(\alpha)=120\alpha \qquad D(\alpha)=15\alpha^{2}+200$
where $\alpha$ is in degrees. The efficiency ratio is:

$$R(\alpha)=\frac{L(\alpha)}{D(\alpha)}=\frac{120\alpha}{15\alpha^{2}+200}$$

Determine the angle of attack $\alpha$ that maximizes $R(\alpha)$. Show all work.

What is the Perfect Wing Problem?

The Perfect Wing Problem is a classic optimization question used in aerodynamics that finds at what angle of attack is an airplane wing most efficient. It's about finding the exact angle where the wing produces the most lift while creating the least drag. This angle is important because it represents the point where the wing is doing its job in the most efficient way possible.

The reason this problem is very popular is because it uses the ideas of optimization in aviation using a model that is simple enough to study but realistic enough to use. Lift and drag don't increase at the same rate and because of this difference, there is always one angle where the ratio between them reaches a maximum. That angle is what pilots and engineers need, and it's what calculus is used to find.

What does a wing do?

An airplane wing is a machine designed to create lift, which is the upward force that makes an airplane fly off the ground and stay in the air. When the plane moves forward, air flows over and under the wing. Because of the wing's curved shape, the air on top moves faster than the air underneath, creating lower pressure above the wing and higher pressure below it. This pressure difference produces lift. Without enough lift, a plane cannot take off or stay airborne.

At the same time, the wing also experiences drag, which is the air resistance against the aircraft as it moves forward. Drag works against the plane's motion and makes the engines work harder. Too much drag wastes fuel and reduces efficiency, so engineers try to design wings that create a lot of lift while keeping drag as low as possible.

Angle of Attack

A major variable that affects both lift and drag is the angle of attack, which is the angle between the wing and the oncoming airflow. When the angle of attack increases, the wing hits the air straight on. This usually causes lift to increase at a steady rate but drag increases much faster so if the angle becomes too high, the airflow can no longer stay attached to the wing, causing a stall.

Lift-to-Drag Ratio

To measure how efficiently a wing is performing, engineers use the lift to drag ratio. This ratio compares how much lift the wing produces to how much drag it experiences. A higher lift to drag ratio means the wing is producing a strong upward force while losing less energy to air resistance. This is important for fuel efficiency during long flights. Pilots and engineers try to fly at or close to the angle of attack that gives the maximum lift to drag ratio, because that is where the wing is working at its best.

What's being maximized?

In this optimization problem, the quantity being maximized is the lift to drag ratio, which is a measure of how efficiently an airplane wing works at a specific angle of attack.

The reason we maximize this ratio is that lift and drag do not increase at the same rate when the angle of attack changes. As the angle of attack increases, lift increases in a mostly linear way, but drag increases way faster and curves upward. This means that at low angles, the wing does not produce enough lift, and at high angles, drag becomes too large. Somewhere in between, there is an angle where the wing is working at its highest efficiency. By using calculus to maximize the lift to drag ratio, we can find this exact angle.

In real aviation, pilots and engineers use this value because it represents the most fuel efficient and aerodynamically effective way for an aircraft to fly during cruise. Optimization with Calculus

The Problem: A wing's lift and drag are modeled by $L(\alpha)=120\alpha$ and $D(\alpha)=15\alpha^{2}+200$ where $\alpha$ is the angle of attack in degrees. The aerodynamic efficiency is the lift-to-drag ratio $R(\alpha)=L/D$. Find the angle $\alpha$ that maximizes $R(\alpha)$.

  1. Step 1 — Define the Problem

    We are maximizing the lift-to-drag ratio $R(\alpha)$, which measures how efficiently the wing produces lift relative to the drag it experiences.

  2. Step 2 — Function and Domain
    $$R(\alpha)=\frac{120\alpha}{15\alpha^{2}+200},\qquad 0<\alpha<\alpha_{\text{stall}}$$

    The domain is restricted to positive angles below the stall angle (roughly $15^\circ$–$20^\circ$ for typical wings).

  3. Step 3 — Differentiate

    Apply the quotient rule $\displaystyle\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)'=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^{2}}$ with $u=120\alpha$, $v=15\alpha^{2}+200$:

    $$\begin{aligned} R'(\alpha)&=\frac{120\,(15\alpha^{2}+200)-120\alpha\,(30\alpha)}{(15\alpha^{2}+200)^{2}}\\[4pt] &=\frac{1800\alpha^{2}+24000-3600\alpha^{2}}{(15\alpha^{2}+200)^{2}}\\[4pt] &=\frac{24000-1800\alpha^{2}}{(15\alpha^{2}+200)^{2}} \end{aligned}$$
  4. Step 4 — Critical Numbers

    Set $R'(\alpha)=0$. The denominator is always positive, so the numerator must be zero:

    $$\begin{aligned} 24000-1800\alpha^{2}&=0\\ 1800\alpha^{2}&=24000\\ \alpha^{2}&=\frac{24000}{1800}=\frac{40}{3}\\[4pt] \alpha^{*}&=\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}\approx 3.65^\circ \end{aligned}$$
    The only critical point in the domain is $\displaystyle\boxed{\alpha^{*}=\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}\approx 3.65^\circ}$.
  5. Step 5 — Confirm Maximum (Second Derivative Test)

    Differentiate $R'(\alpha)$. Let $N(\alpha)=24000-1800\alpha^{2}$ and $D(\alpha)=(15\alpha^{2}+200)^{2}$. Then $N'=-3600\alpha$ and $D'=60\alpha(15\alpha^{2}+200)$. Using the quotient rule again:

    $$\begin{aligned} R''(\alpha)&=\frac{N'D-ND'}{D^{2}} \end{aligned}$$

    At $\alpha^{*}$ we have $N(\alpha^{*})=0$, so the $ND'$ term vanishes:

    $$\begin{aligned} R''(\alpha^{*})&=\frac{-3600\alpha^{*}\,(15\alpha^{*2}+200)^{2}}{(15\alpha^{*2}+200)^{4}}\\[4pt] &=\frac{-3600\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}}{(15\cdot\frac{40}{3}+200)^{2}}\\[4pt] &=\frac{-3600\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}}{(200+200)^{2}}\\[4pt] &=\frac{-3600\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}}{160000}\\[4pt] &=-\frac{9}{400}\sqrt{\frac{40}{3}}<0 \end{aligned}$$

    Since $R''(\alpha^{*})<0$, the function is concave down at the critical point, confirming a maximum.

    The maximum lift-to- drag ratio is $\displaystyle R_{\max}=R(\alpha^{*})\approx 1.10$, achieved at $\displaystyle\boxed{\alpha^{*}=3.65^\circ}$.

Conclusion: At an angle of attack of approximately $3.65^\circ$, the wing produces the highest lift relative to its drag — this is the most aerodynamically efficient cruising angle for the given wing model, meaning the aircraft can fly farthest on the least fuel.
